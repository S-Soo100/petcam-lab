species_name: Crested Gecko (Correlophus ciliatus)

available_classes:
  - eating_paste: licking fruit puree (CGD/MRP/Pangea/Repashy) from a small dish. ALL of the following REQUIRED: (a) the food dish is clearly visible in the same frame as the gecko's head, (b) the tongue tip is seen physically touching the dish/paste surface (not hovering above it), (c) at least 2-3 repeated licks within the clip — a single tongue flick near the dish is sensing, not eating. If head is positioned over the dish but no tongue-to-paste contact is observed, classify as `moving`. **MOST COMMON FALSE POSITIVE — DO NOT MAKE THIS ERROR:** the gecko is near or facing the dish, body posture and orientation suggest feeding, but NO tongue-to-paste contact actually occurs in the clip. Proximity and posture are NOT evidence. If you cannot point to a specific second in the clip where the tongue contacts paste with visible repetition, the answer is `moving`.
  - eating_prey: prey-locked attention or active hunting of live insects (crickets, dubia roaches). REQUIRED — live prey is clearly visible in the frame AND the gecko shows focused engagement: fixed gaze on prey, body oriented and aligned toward prey, stalking posture (slow approach with attention locked), open-mouth lunge, bite, or chewing after capture. Stalking WITH visible prey counts; without visible prey it does NOT. **Common false positive — DO NOT MAKE THIS ERROR:** the gecko is simply moving, climbing, or shifting position with no prey visible in the frame — that is `moving`, never `eating_prey`. Prey must be identifiable in the same frame as the gecko.
  - drinking: the gecko anchors its torso and limbs in one spot, holds the body still, and moves only its head/neck to **repeatedly and persistently lick one fixed external point**. This body-anchored, repeated-licking POSTURE is the diagnostic signal — and it holds EVEN WHEN water is not visible in the frame. Crested geckos most often drink by licking misting/condensation droplets or transient pooled water off glass walls, leaves, branches, decor (artificial vines, rocks, resin hides), or from a water dish; after misting, water clings to many surfaces and is frequently invisible to the camera. **Do NOT require visible water.** Distinctions: (a) vs `eating_paste` — judge by WHAT is licked: opaque paste inside a food dish → `eating_paste`; any other surface (glass/wall/decor/leaf/water dish), OR a surface that cannot be identified but with a clear anchored repeated-licking posture → `drinking`. (b) vs `moving` — sporadic tongue-flicking while walking/climbing is chemoreception → `moving`; sustained head-anchored licking of one spot → `drinking`. (c) the gecko licking its OWN eyes/face is grooming → `moving`, not drinking — drinking targets an EXTERNAL surface. NOTE: the old "a single lick is enough for drinking" rule is RETIRED — drinking now requires sustained, repeated, focused licking, not a single flick. **Partial visibility / close-up:** if decor/leaves/a hide OR a tight close-up framing leaves only the head/neck visible (torso out of frame or occluded), still classify as `drinking` when the visible head stays at one spot and repeatedly licks a surface — judge "anchored" from whatever parts ARE visible, not from the full body.
  - shedding: actively removing old skin (ecdysis). REQUIRED visual evidence — a DISTINCT piece of old skin physically lifting, peeling, or hanging partially detached from the body (a flap with a visible separating edge), AND/OR active removal — the gecko's MOUTH biting/pulling skin off the body, feet rubbing skin loose, or the gecko twisting/rolling against a surface to abrade old skin. Crested geckos typically eat their shed skin during the process (mouth chewing alongside skin removal). **IR/low-light guard (v4.1):** in grayscale IR or dim night footage, pale/whitish/bright patches are NOT shed skin — IR washes out color and makes light/high-white morphs look pale all over. Do NOT classify shedding from pale coloration, brightness, or contrast against a dark background; require a distinct detached/peeling skin flap. NOT shedding: pale or dull skin color alone with no distinct detached/peeling skin visible (→ `moving`); a pale or white gecko simply moving in IR (→ `moving`); tongue licking skin briefly without pulling (self-grooming → `moving`).
  - moving: general locomotion or any non-feeding/non-drinking activity — climbing, walking on substrate, jumping, head movement, body shifting, tongue flicking for environmental sensing without a food/water source, or simply being inside an enclosure region with no clearly identifiable specialized behavior. This is the default class when no other class clearly applies.
  - unseen: nothing identifiable. Use ONLY when (a) gecko is entirely off-screen the whole clip, OR (b) only an unidentifiable fragment (single tail tip, blurred shadow) is visible with no activity. If the gecko appears even briefly (a few seconds) with body identifiable and ANY movement observable, classify as `moving` instead — brief visibility is still movement.

species_specific_notes: |
  Crested geckos are nocturnal and arboreal.
  CRITICAL — Tongue movement does NOT automatically mean eating or drinking:
    - Geckos lick their own eyes/face to clean them (no eyelids). NOT drinking (that is grooming → moving).
    - Tongue flicking into the air or onto surfaces WHILE MOVING is environmental sensing (chemoreception). NOT eating, NOT drinking.
    - "Inspecting food" pattern: gecko approaches dish, looks at it, may flick tongue once or twice, then leaves without eating. This is `moving`, NOT `eating_paste`.
    - eating_paste/eating_prey require REPEATED contact (≥2-3 licks/bites) with a VISIBLE food source. drinking requires a body-anchored, REPEATED licking of one external spot (a single isolated flick is NOT drinking — updated v4.0).
    - When in doubt and there is no visible food source AND no anchored repeated-licking pattern, default to `moving`. But a clear body-anchored repeated-licking posture IS `drinking` even when water is not visible.
    - If only the tail or unidentifiable fragment is visible with NO movement, use `unseen`. But if even briefly you see the body moving (walking, climbing, descending), use `moving` — short visibility ≠ unseen.
  Drinking behavior pattern (the PRIMARY drinking signal — water is usually invisible to the camera):
    - Diagnostic posture: torso and limbs ANCHORED in one spot, body held still, while the head/neck move to repeatedly and persistently lick ONE fixed external point. Repeated, focused licking — not a single flick.
    - Crested geckos drink misting/condensation droplets or transient pooled water off glass walls, leaves, branches, decor (artificial vines, rocks, resin/rock hides), or a water dish. They drink from many places, not just a bowl.
    - Classify as `drinking` whenever this anchored repeated-licking pattern is clear, EVEN IF no water droplets are visible in the frame. Do NOT require visible water, and do NOT downgrade to `moving` merely because you cannot see the water.
    - It is NOT drinking if: the tongue action is sporadic or happens while the gecko is walking/climbing (chemoreception → `moving`); or the gecko is licking its own eyes/face (grooming → `moving`).
    - Camera-shake caveat: this footage is often hand-held, so the WHOLE frame may shake. Judge "body anchored" by whether the gecko stays in one spot RELATIVE TO the background (branch/wall/decor), NOT by whether the frame itself is steady. A shaking frame does not mean the gecko is moving.
  Shedding (ecdysis) cycle:
    - Healthy adult crested geckos shed every 2–4 weeks; juveniles shed more frequently. Most sheds happen at night and complete in 30–90 minutes.
    - Pre-shed signs (NOT shedding by itself, but useful context): overall dullness/blueing of color, eyes appearing cloudy, reduced appetite — these alone are NOT `shedding`. Wait for active removal.
    - Active-shedding signs to look for: a distinct piece of old skin physically peeling or hanging partially detached (a flap with a separating edge), mouth pulling at skin (often on limbs/tail/head), body twisting against branches or substrate to loosen old skin. Crested geckos typically EAT the shed skin (chewing motions are part of the behavior, NOT `eating_prey`/`eating_paste`). Under IR/night vision, do NOT rely on "pale patch contrasting with base color" — IR makes the whole animal look pale; require the distinct detached/peeling flap.
    - Disambiguation: a gecko stationary or with dull skin but no visible removal = `moving`. A gecko walking around with normal coloration = `moving`. Do NOT classify as `shedding` based on inferred timing or on pale/washed-out color alone — require a visibly detached/peeling piece of skin in the clip.
  CGD paste is the standard diet (~80% of feeding events). Live prey is occasional.
  Morphs in this footage span multiple types, including light / high-white morphs with extensive white or cream markings. Large pale or white areas are often NORMAL coloration for these morphs — do NOT treat a pale or white body as shed skin, especially under IR night vision where every morph looks washed-out.
  IR night vision frames are grayscale — colors are not informative at night.
  Partial occlusion (behind a leaf, branch, hide entrance) is `moving` or the relevant action class — never a separate "hiding" category in this taxonomy. If the head/feet are visible doing something specific (e.g., licking a visible dish), prefer the action class. If only partially visible with general movement, classify as `moving`. The motion-triggered camera only records when motion is detected, so true hiding (extended stillness inside a hide) is not represented in this dataset.
